THE NOW

If (the year is 2017) {
Define: Science Fiction; Fiction based on imagined future scientific or technological advances and major social or environmental changes, frequently portraying space or time travel and life on other planets.
}

The future ultimately becomes the present. We are in the present, so aren't we in the “future?”

“O brave new world ⁄ That has such people in 't!” Such people and such...robots? Such AI? Was Miranda so clairvoyantly referring to the diversity of beings that inhabit and challenge our understanding of “people,” of “humans?” Our existence as modern human beings is inextricable with those living around us as well as the technological realm we are submerged in. Humans are social creatures; it seems natural that we would create something, someone even, to quell our hunger for company. Social media was the entrance to this intangible world of connection. Facebook allows us to connect with “real” people, our friends across the street, the country, the world. But they are still people who we're interacting with.

Except how does Facebook recognize that face? How is that your friend suggestions are actually people you know? According to Yann LeCun, the Director at Facebook AI Research, “if we want to understand ourselves, we have to understand what intelligence is all about.” Intelligence in the form of machines, whose code mimics the neural networks of our own brains. Though this concept may seem like it's been pulled from a science fiction novel, it is our reality. Now.

“When the past is always with you, it may as well be present; and if it is present, it will be future as well” (id= “William Gibson”).

Human-esque AI's do not exist solely within the pages of Neuromancer or Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep. Jules, for example, is a robot whose sophisticated AI program enables him to have conversations. Not only is he “smart,” he unnervingly resembles a balding human male in his mid 30's. “Resemblance” may become an obsolete description; ultimately there will not be a relevant enough difference between humans and AI, especially as they look more and more like “us,” as they merge with “us.”

"No. She couldn't imagine what I'd be like” (259).

But I would argue that they, the authors of so many sci-fi novels could, did, imagine what they, AI, would be like. Almost exactly. We are living out those pages, the world of those words. William Gibson. Philip K Dick. Isaac Asimov.

"The saddest aspect of life right now is that science fiction gathers knowledge faster than society gathers wisdom" (id= “Isaac Asimov”).

“Neuromancer was immortality” (259). And so is imagination, technology and philosophy. One cannot exist without the others.

Neuromancer, the paradigm of AI. “His” existence will transcend time, his “immortality” a display of power. Neuromancer is only the inception of AI and what's to come. In our time, in our future, AI will become more sophisticated, more ubiquitous, more immortal.
